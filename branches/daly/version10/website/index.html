Magnus

A System for Exploring Infinite Groups
Magnus is a special purpose computer algebra system designed to carry out experiments with infinite groups. These infinite groups come, in the main, equipped with finite presentations. There are 3 canonical classes of problems ( the Word Problem, the Conjugacy Problem, and the Isomorphism Problem) which have been shown to have no general algorithmic solution for finitely presented groups. Occasionally a fourth problem, the Subgroup Problem), is cited. However, given a particular group it is often possible to obtain a solution. There are many procedures which may or may not terminate with a solution.

Magnus is designed to take an experimental approach to working with these finitely presented groups. The philosophy is to consider a group as an object to which we can apply a range of procedures to extract properties of the group. For example if we can find a canonical decomposition of a group then we can conclude that the group, as given by the finite presentation, is free abelian.

Magnus has a novel approach for interactions called a zero-learning curve interface. Since almost nothing is known about a given group the user is free to experiment by choosing tools to apply to the group. All menus and help are context sensitive. The groups are presented on a "workbench" where they can be manipulate, probed, and the results can be cataloged for later use.

Abelian Group Questions gives a general discussion of some of the questions and definitions that arise. From here you can follow some examples of how to answer such questions using Magnus.

A group G is said to be an amalgamated product of its subgroups A and B with the subgroup H amalgamated, if (i) G is generated by A and B, termed the factors; (ii) A and B intersect in H, termed the amalgamated subgroup; (iii) every product of the form c_1 c_2 ... c_n, where c_i is not the identity, each c_i is in the union of A and B, and consecutive terms c_i lie in diferent factors, is different from the identity.
