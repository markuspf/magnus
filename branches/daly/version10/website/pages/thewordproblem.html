The Word Problem

Given a finite presentation P of a group G and given two words U, V defining elements u, v of G, the Word Problem is to decide if U is congruent to V. There is no general algorithm which, given P, U and V, can decide this question.

There is no general algorithm to solve this problem for all finite presentations. Magnus has several algorithms which can be applied to a finite presentation to attempt to decide this question for a particular finite presentation.

The Word Problem was defined by M. Dehn in 1910. The idea was to find algorithms for important presentations. When the idea of an algorithm was formalized in the 1930's it could be proven that the word problem had no general algorithm and was thus unsolvable.

Cejtin[1] proposed this simple semigroup example which is known to have an unsolveable word problem:

<a,b,c,d,e | ac=ca, ad=da, bc=cb, bd=db, ce=eca, de=edb, cca=ccae>


Cejtin proposed a semigroup presentation which illustrates the individual word problem. Given an arbitrary word w and the presentation:

<a,b,c,d,e | ac=ca, ad=da, bc=cb, bd=db, ce=eca, de=edb, cca=ccae, cdca=cdcae, caaa=aaa, daaa=aaa>

does the word w = aaa?
