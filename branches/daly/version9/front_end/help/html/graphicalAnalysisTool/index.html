THE GRAPHICAL ANALYSIS TOOL


The Graphical Analysis Tool will construct various graphical presentations of the given data set. These will include a histogram, a frequency polygon, and a normal distribution with the computed mean and standard deviation superimposed on the histogram. The default graph will be a histogram automatically constructed with eight equally spaced classes. The user can change either the graph type or the class settings in the output box.

An extremely useful tool in analyzing data is to make a graphical presentation of the frequency distribution. This will help to determine if the data is symmetrical or not and will aid in choosing a mathematical model for the underlying population. These graphical techniques are often considered part of exploratory data analysis which are those techniques used in understanding data and in determining appropriate models.

The two most common types of graphical presentations used with frequency distributions are the histogram and the frequency polygon. They both convey the same information and therefore which one is used in any particular analysis is really the preference of the user. A stem and leaf plot is another type of graphical presentation which is related to the two above.

A histogram is a bar graph of a frequency distribution. The horizontal axis is the measurement scale while the vertical axis will represent the frequencies. There will be a bar for each class. The width of the bar will be the class interval while the height of the bar will be the class frequency. The shape of the histogram will indicate the symmetry or skewness (lack of symmetry) of the data. This will give us a clue to the shape of the overall population distribution.

The second type of graphical presentation is called a frequency polygon . This is a line graph of a frequency distrbution. As in the histogram the horizontal axis will represent the measurements while the vertical axis will be the frequencies. Points will be plotted at the intersections of each class mark and its corresponding class frequency. These points will then be connected by straight line segments. It is common practice to include two additional points, one prior to the first class and one after the last class, with frequencies of zero. When this is done the total areas enclosed by a histogram of a frequency distribution and a frequency polygon of the same frequency distribution will be the same. It is often important to determine if a given data set is normally distributed or not. A quick but not statistically rigorous graphical procedure would be to superimpose a normal distribution with the computed mean and standard deviation on the histogram.
